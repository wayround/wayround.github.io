Somaliland: a country that doesn't exist

Islam, Conflict

 

It’s a warm winter afternoon in Hargeisa, capital of the unrecognized country of Somaliland. The sky above is blue and empty, stretching monotonously from horizon to horizon. Set into its blank background, the sun shimmers like a diamond. But you don’t get diamonds without bloodshed.

A quarter of a century ago Somalia’s brutal dictator, Siad Barre, was overthrown in a bloody coup. The power vacuum left by Barre’s demise was filled with local militias, squabbling over the remains of the failed state. In the south, this civil war still rages. But in northern Somalia things have calmed down. Somaliland, a former British protectorate, is an island of peace and stability in what is otherwise a conflict-ridden region.

In Hargeisa’s sprawling, outdoor suq, or market, I’m doing two things that visitors are told to never do: exchange money and discuss politics.

Bricks of currency as tall as children are placed on blue tarps. Money-changers sit behind their piles of cash, smiling with teeth stained red by chewing khat, a stimulant similar to tobacco. Some English is spoken here, and I go from stall to stall, trying to find which one has the best rate.

Near the beginning of the vegetable portion of the suq, I find the guy I want to do business with. As is customary for any large transaction, bargaining is expected. I sit down on the tarp in front of the exchanger. I’m offered khat, but I politely refuse.

We spend about fifteen minutes haggling. My request for $100 worth of Somaliland Shillings is enough to fill a wheelbarrow. He doesn’t understand why I would want that many Shillings. After all, they’re only useful for micro-transactions. I wanted to tell him about my interest in Somalia’s history, in its economic mismanagement and hyperinflation. But instead I just tell him that the money is ‘for a friend’. That seems to answer all of his questions.

A group of people have collected behind me on the street, amused and curious about the foreigner purchasing an obscene amount of local currency. Two men sit down next to me, one on either side, and we all shake hands.

‘You are from America, yes?’ one of the men asks.

‘Yes, I am,’ I reply, nodding and smiling. Most Americans in this part of the world wouldn’t confess that they’re from the USA. But long ago I’d learned that the ‘Clash of Civilizations’ between the West and the Islamic World was just a myth: only the radicals on both sides--Fox News and Al Qaeda, respectively--actually believe a word of it.

‘Obama or Bush?’ the second man asked.

This multiple-choice question had only one correct answer. I gave my reply.

‘Oh yes, very good’, he responded. And then raised his voice and repeated ‘Obama!’ so that the crowd would know my answer.

‘I would like to apologize for the drones,’ I said, acting in my capacity as de facto cultural ambassador.

The Obama administration has recently expanded its drone program in the Horn of Africa and Yemen, something that the region’s inhabitants universally despise. Somaliland, though relatively peaceful, is still stuck right between those two conflicts. Everyone reads the papers and knows about the bombings. They were all fearful of the day when drones would start circling over Hargeisa.

‘It is OK, please have some tea,’ the money-changer said. Tea is always close at hand in this part of the world. I was handed a cup of spicy, milky chai before I could even respond.

I wanted to know the local opinion about the Saudi invasion of Yemen. Saudi Arabia is deeply entangled in the politics of this region, though usually the country gets its way with economic bullying; actual military intervention is unheard of.

’It’s very very bad’, the money lender told me. I hadn’t expected this. To me, it seemed like the Saudi invasion was another blatant example of Sunnis squashing dissent among Houthi Shias. But the locals didn’t see it that way. Their concerns were much more secular. Somaliland’s government always boasts about its ‘deep water port’ and ‘livestock production’--what would Saudi Arabia do if it laid its eyes on Somaliland’s two most precious assets?

After the conversation ended I stood up and said my farewells. I left the suq laden with two backpacks full of Somaliland Shillings.

 

Written by Cameron Smith
